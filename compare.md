# How MCB Compares to Other Clinical Evidence Tools

The clinical evidence market has changed fast. In the last six months, NEJM Journal Watch rebranded to NEJM Clinician and cut its price. UpToDate launched Expert AI. Heidi Evidence arrived free for UK doctors. OpenEvidence reached a $12 billion valuation. ChatGPT for Clinicians went live in the US.

Every one of these is a good product. None of them does the same thing as the others. This page explains what each tool is built for and where The Monday Clinical Brief sits in that landscape.

## NEJM Clinician (formerly Journal Watch)

NEJM Journal Watch rebranded to **NEJM Clinician** in November 2025, with a redesigned digital platform and broader clinical coverage. The price for UK subscribers dropped to approximately **£75 per year** — down from the previous premium tier.

**What changed:** The rebrand added deeper clinical context, practical guidance, and broader coverage beyond the traditional Journal Watch expert picks. The editorial model — senior clinicians selecting and commenting on important papers — remains the core strength.

**How MCB differs:**

NEJM Clinician gives you **expert picks** — a small number of papers selected by named editors who explain why each one matters. MCB gives you **every article** from the top five journals in your specialty. The approaches serve different needs.

If you want editorial judgment and commentary from named experts, NEJM Clinician is strong. If you want completeness — not missing anything published in your field — MCB covers more ground.

On price: even with NEJM's reduction to £75, MCB is still less than a third of the cost at £20/year. NEJM Clinician charges per specialty track; MCB charges per specialty at the same flat rate. If you follow two specialties, the gap widens.

**Verdict:** Strong product, credible brand. MCB is cheaper, broader, and more UK-contextualised. NEJM Clinician is deeper on editorial commentary. Many doctors use both.

## UpToDate (and Expert AI)

UpToDate remains the dominant point-of-care clinical reference. In 2025–26, Wolters Kluwer added **Expert AI** — a generative AI layer that lets you ask natural-language questions and receive UpToDate-grounded answers.

**The UK caveat:** UpToDate Expert AI (the AI assistant) is currently **available only in the US and Canada** for personal subscriptions. Select Enterprise Edition accounts in Australia, Hong Kong, and a handful of other countries have access, but individual UK subscribers cannot yet use Expert AI. Standard UpToDate remains available in the UK, and most NHS trusts provide institutional access.

**How MCB differs:**

UpToDate and MCB are different products. UpToDate is a reference you consult when a patient is in front of you. MCB is a digest you read on Monday morning to stay current with what was published last week. They do not compete — they complement.

If your trust provides UpToDate access, use it at the point of care. Use MCB to stay current with the primary literature so you know when UpToDate's recommendations have been updated (or should be).

**Verdict:** Essential reference tool, but not a journal digest. Expert AI is not available to UK individuals. MCB fills a different gap entirely.

## Heidi Evidence

Launched February 2026. Free for UK clinicians. Powered by Claude. Partnered with NICE, BMJ Group, HealthPathways, and MIMS. Strengthened by the acquisition of UK-based AutoMedica and participation in the MHRA AI Airlock pilot.

**What it does:** Heidi Evidence is a clinical evidence lookup tool. You ask a clinical question; it returns a cited answer grounded in UK-relevant sources. It sits inside the broader Heidi platform (which also includes AI clinical documentation).

**How MCB differs:**

Heidi is **pull** — you ask, it answers. MCB is **push** — it tells you what's new before you ask. These are fundamentally different behaviours.

Heidi protects against not knowing the answer when a question comes up. MCB protects against not knowing the question exists — surfacing papers you would never have searched for because you did not know they had been published.

Heidi has no weekly reading cadence, no CPD logging, and no systematic journal coverage. It is not trying to keep you current. It is trying to keep you safe at the point of care.

**Verdict:** Excellent free tool for consultation-room lookups. Does not replace a weekly digest. Use both. [Read the full comparison](/blog/journal-summary-subscription/heidi-evidence-vs-weekly-journal-digest/)

## OpenEvidence

OpenEvidence raised $250 million in January 2026 at a $12 billion valuation. It offers AI-powered clinical reasoning with direct access to NEJM content, plus newer features including DeepConsult (multi-step clinical reasoning) and Coding Intelligence.

**The UK caveat:** OpenEvidence is **currently US-only**. UK expansion has been signalled but no launch date is confirmed. When it arrives, it will likely be free and ad-supported.

**How MCB differs:**

OpenEvidence is building a workflow platform — evidence search, documentation, coding, communications. MCB is a weekly email. The ambitions are different by orders of magnitude.

For UK doctors today, OpenEvidence is not yet available. When it launches in the UK, it will be a pull/lookup tool (like Heidi but with deeper agentic capabilities). It will not be a weekly digest. The same push-vs-pull distinction applies.

The ad-supported model is worth noting. MCB has no advertising, no sponsored content, and no pharmaceutical partnerships. Every paper is included because it was published in a top-5 journal, not because someone paid to surface it.

**Verdict:** Not yet available in the UK. When it arrives, it will serve a different use case. MCB's ad-free model and UK-native positioning are the key differentiators.

## ChatGPT for Clinicians

Launched 22 April 2026. Free for verified US physicians, nurse practitioners, physician assistants, and pharmacists. Features include clinical search with citations, reusable workflow skills, deep medical research, documentation assistance, and CME credits.

**The UK caveat:** ChatGPT for Clinicians is **US-only** at launch. UK availability has not been announced.

**How MCB differs:**

ChatGPT for Clinicians is a general-purpose clinical AI assistant. It can do many things — search evidence, generate documents, support research. A weekly journal digest is one narrow task that a general-purpose tool could theoretically do but in practice does not do well, because the value of a digest is editorial curation, not retrieval.

A curated digest tells you: "of the 47 papers published in your specialty this week, these are the ones worth reading." A general-purpose AI can retrieve papers, but it does not monitor journals on a weekly schedule, filter by your specialty's top-5 journals, or format the output for UK CPD logging.

**Verdict:** Powerful but US-only. When available in the UK, it will be a clinical assistant, not a current-awareness tool. MCB serves a different job.

## The Bottom Line

The market now has excellent tools for **looking things up** (UpToDate, Heidi Evidence, OpenEvidence, ChatGPT for Clinicians). These are consultation-room tools.

What the market has fewer of is tools for **staying current** — the Monday morning problem of "what was published last week that I need to know about?" That is the job MCB is built for.

At £20 a year, covering 31 specialties, formatted for UK CPD, and delivered by email every Monday — MCB is the cheapest and broadest way to solve the current-awareness problem. Use the lookup tools alongside it. They are complementary, not competitive.
