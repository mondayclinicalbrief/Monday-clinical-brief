JAMA psychiatry

Dialectical Behavior Therapy vs Schema Therapy for Patients With Borderline Personality Disorder: The BOOTS Multicenter Randomized Clinical Trial

Wibbelink CJM, Kamphuis JH, Sinnaeve R et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Multicenter randomized controlled trial
Key Question: How does dialectical behavior therapy (DBT) compare to schema therapy (ST) for effectiveness in treating borderline personality disorder?
Key Findings:
  • No significant difference between DBT and ST in reducing BPD severity over 3 years (p=0.27, Cohen's d=0.15, 95% CI -0.17 to 0.47)
  • Both treatments showed large, substantial improvements across all outcome measures including BPD symptoms, functioning, and quality of life
  • Dropout rates were similar between groups (DBT: 52% at 2 years; ST: 46% at 2 years)
Clinical Relevance: This provides robust evidence that both DBT and ST are equally effective for BPD treatment, giving UK clinicians flexibility in choosing between these evidence-based approaches based on patient preferences, therapist expertise, or service capacity.
Limitations: High dropout rates (approximately 50% at 2 years) limit the generalizability of findings and reflect real-world treatment challenges in BPD populations.

JAMA psychiatry

Antipsychotic Efficacy and Safety of LB-102 in the Treatment of Adults With Acute Schizophrenia: A Randomized Clinical Trial

Eramo A, Correll CU, Walling DP et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Randomized controlled trial (phase 2, double-blind, placebo-controlled)
Key Question: Is LB-102 (N-methyl amisulpride), a novel benzamide antipsychotic, effective and safe for treating acute schizophrenia in hospitalized adults?
Key Findings:
  • LB-102 50mg and 75mg demonstrated statistically superior efficacy versus placebo on PANSS total scores at 4 weeks (mean reductions: placebo -9.3, 50mg -14.3, 75mg -14.0; effect sizes 0.61 and 0.41 respectively)
  • Treatment-emergent adverse events occurred in 56-75% of participants across groups, with 10 withdrawals due to adverse events and 5 serious adverse events including one death in placebo group
  • 100mg dose showed largest effect size (0.83) but was underpowered with only 36 participants
Clinical Relevance: This provides early evidence for a potentially new antipsychotic option for acute schizophrenia management in UK inpatient settings, though regulatory approval and NICE evaluation would be required.
Limitations: Phase 2 study with relatively short 4-week treatment duration limits assessment of longer-term efficacy and safety profile.

Genetic overlap and shared risk loci between autism spectrum disorder and cardiometabolic traits

Muntané G, Shadrin A, Guardiola-Ripoll M et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Genome-wide association study analysis using statistical modelling approaches
Key Question: What is the shared genetic architecture between autism spectrum disorder and cardiometabolic traits?
Key Findings:
  • Significant polygenic overlap identified between ASD and cardiometabolic phenotypes despite negligible overall genetic correlation
  • Positive genetic correlations found within shared components for ASD with metabolic traits (BMI rg=0.03, T2DM rg=0.23, cholesterol rg=0.78), but negative correlations with cardiovascular traits (blood pressure rg=-0.22, coronary artery disease rg=-0.90)
  • 100 shared genetic loci identified between ASD and cardiometabolic traits, mapping to 124 genes with potential therapeutic relevance
Clinical Relevance: These findings provide genetic evidence for the well-recognised cardiometabolic comorbidities in ASD patients, potentially informing risk stratification and personalised prevention strategies in NHS autism services.
Limitations: The study relies on statistical associations from GWAS data without functional validation of identified genetic variants.

Key Findings:

  • Disease-associated cellular states emerge with characteristic dysregulated gene expression programs

Molecular psychiatry

Multimodal microglial and kynurenine pathway alterations across the affective-psychosis spectrum: a systematic review of patterns, heterogeneity, and dimensional implications

Nussbaumer M, Guest PC, Schiltz K et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Systematic review with quantitative synthesis
Key Question: What are the patterns of microglial activation and kynurenine pathway alterations across major depressive disorder, bipolar disorder, and schizophrenia?
Key Findings:
  • MDD showed most consistent evidence with increased TSPO-PET binding in frontolimbic regions (cingulate, hippocampus, prefrontal cortex) and lower heterogeneity than schizophrenia
  • Schizophrenia demonstrated heterogeneous TSPO findings but consistent shift toward KYNA branch of kynurenine pathway (increased CSF and cortical KYNA), supporting glutamatergic dysregulation
  • Bipolar disorder evidence was limited, with single TSPO-PET study showing hippocampal increases and subgroup effects emerging with psychosis/suicide stratification
Clinical Relevance: This supports moving away from categorical diagnostic approaches toward dimensional phenotyping and biological subgroup identification in NHS psychiatric services, particularly for treatment-resistant cases.
Limitations: Limited cellular specificity of TSPO-PET imaging, small sample sizes, and postmortem studies restricted to cortical/limbic regions rather than whole-brain analysis.

Molecular psychiatry

Shared and disorder-specific prenatal and perinatal risk factors for neurodevelopmental disorders: a nationwide cohort study

Peyre H, Rios P, Botton J et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Nationwide cohort study
Key Question: What prenatal and perinatal risk factors are shared versus disorder-specific across major neurodevelopmental disorders?
Key Findings:
  • Among 6.8 million French children, 7.5% developed at least one NDD over 9.6 years median follow-up, with high co-occurrence rates (ID 54.5%, ASD 52.2%, ADHD 34.9%)
  • Common risk factors across disorders included male sex, prematurity, small for gestational age, congenital malformations, maternal obesity, and prenatal substance exposure
  • Intellectual disability showed strongest disorder-specific associations with extreme prematurity, severe growth restriction, neonatal hypoxia, and advanced parental age
Clinical Relevance: This large-scale study provides evidence-based risk stratification data that could inform early identification and prevention strategies for neurodevelopmental disorders in UK clinical practice.
Limitations: The study's generalizability to UK populations may be limited given differences in healthcare systems and population characteristics between France and the UK.

Molecular psychiatry

IL-15 receptor alpha deficiency triggers depressive-like behaviors via enhanced microglial synapse pruning

Tang Y, Huang Y, Pan J et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Preclinical experimental study using knockout mouse model with single-nucleus RNA sequencing
Key Question: Does IL-15 receptor alpha deficiency contribute to depression through microglial-mediated synaptic changes?
Key Findings:
  • IL-15RA knockout mice showed depressive-like behaviours with cell-type-specific transcriptomic changes affecting synapse assembly in prefrontal cortex
  • Enhanced CX3CL1/CX3CR1 signalling drove microglial activation and excessive synaptic pruning in these mice
  • CX3CR1 antagonist treatment reversed both depressive behaviours and pathological synaptic pruning
Clinical Relevance: Identifies a specific neuroimmune pathway (IL-15RA/CX3CL1-CX3CR1) that could inform novel therapeutic targets for treatment-resistant depression, particularly relevant given NHS emphasis on precision psychiatry approaches.
Limitations: Mouse model findings may not translate directly to human MDD pathophysiology and treatment responses.
