Pulse Oximeter Performance and Skin Pigment: Comparison of 34 Oximeters Using Current and Emerging Regulatory Frameworks

Hughes C, Chen D, Law T et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Laboratory-based controlled desaturation study
Key Question: How do 34 pulse oximeters perform across different skin pigmentations under current and proposed regulatory frameworks?
Key Findings:
  • Using current standards, 82% (28/34) passed 2017 ISO criteria and 65% (22/34) passed 2013 FDA guidance
  • Under proposed stricter regulations, only 62% (21/34) would pass anticipated ISO standards and just 3% (1/34) would pass anticipated FDA criteria
  • Only 11/34 oximeters showed the expected pattern of more positive bias in darker-skinned participants (median difference 1.42% SpO2)
Clinical Relevance: UK anaesthetists should be aware that most pulse oximeters in current use may not meet emerging regulatory standards for skin pigmentation bias, potentially affecting patient safety monitoring across diverse populations.
Limitations: Laboratory conditions with controlled desaturation may not reflect real-world clinical performance and patient acuity.

Anesthesia and analgesia

Influence of the Glutamatergic MS-LH Circuit Activation on Emergence From Sevoflurane Anesthesia in Mice

Wu J, Dai X, Wu H et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Experimental animal study (mice)
Key Question: Does activation of the glutamatergic medial septum to lateral hypothalamus (MSGlu-LH) neural circuit influence emergence from sevoflurane general anaesthesia?
Key Findings:
  • Optogenetic activation of the MSGlu-LH circuit during steady-state sevoflurane anaesthesia promoted behavioural arousal and increased EEG β power (16.8% vs 9.7%, P=0.0065)
  • Circuit inhibition deepened cortical suppression, increasing δ power and decreasing β/γ power significantly
  • During burst-suppression patterns, circuit activation reduced burst-suppression ratio from 77.5% to 59.0% (P=0.0121)
Clinical Relevance: This preclinical research identifies a specific neural pathway that could potentially be targeted to enhance emergence from volatile anaesthesia, which may inform future development of emergence-facilitating interventions.
Limitations: Animal study findings may not translate directly to human anaesthetic practice and clinical emergence patterns.

Anesthesia and analgesia

Sustainable Anesthesia: A Scoping Review of Carbon Reduction Strategies

Moser A, Hammer S, König A et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Scoping review
Key Question: What interventions can reduce carbon emissions from anaesthetic practice without compromising patient safety?
Key Findings:
  • 33 studies across 10 countries demonstrated CO2 equivalent reductions of 50-90% through various sustainability interventions
  • Greatest impact achieved by combining behavioural changes (desflurane elimination, nitrous oxide restriction), technical measures (optimised fresh gas flows, equipment reuse), and organisational strategies (education programmes, telemedicine)
  • Some interventions delivered absolute savings of several tonnes CO2 equivalent per year per department
Clinical Relevance: Directly supports NHS Net Zero commitments by providing evidence-based strategies for reducing anaesthetic carbon footprint while maintaining patient safety standards.
Limitations: Five studies rated as low quality, and heterogeneity of interventions limits direct comparison of effectiveness between strategies.

Study Type: Position statement/clinical guidance
Key Question: How should ambulatory anaesthesia teams assess VTE risk and implement prophylaxis protocols for outpatient surgical patients?
Key Findings:
  • VTE affects up to 900,000 Americans annually with 60,000-100,000 deaths, over half of post-discharge events linked to recent surgery/hospitalisation

Limitations: Position statement represents expert consensus rather than new primary research evidence.

Analgesic Effect of a Thromboxane A2 Receptor Agonist on Multiple Pain Models in Mice

Fujiwara A, Ueno T, Matsumura S et al. · 2026 Apr 23
Study Type: Experimental animal study
Key Question: Does thromboxane A2 receptor (TPR) activation provide analgesia in mouse pain models?
Key Findings:
  • Intrathecal TPR agonist (I-BOP) dose-dependently reduced PGE2-induced allodynia scores to 20% (1ng dose) and 10% (10ng dose) compared to control
  • In neuropathic pain model, I-BOP significantly increased mechanical withdrawal thresholds with dose-dependent effects
  • In formalin test, I-BOP selectively reduced second-phase nociceptive behaviour (140±6.2 vs 623±18.1 seconds control) without affecting first phase
Clinical Relevance: This preclinical work identifies TPR as a novel spinal analgesic target, potentially offering new therapeutic approaches for inflammatory and neuropathic pain conditions in clinical anaesthesia practice.
Limitations: Animal model findings may not translate to human pain physiology and clinical effectiveness.

A population-based cross-sectional analysis of extended-release opioid dispensing incidence, prognostic factors, and variation after total joint arthroplasty

Malone JL, Tierney SL, McGinn RN et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Population-based cross-sectional study
Key Question: What factors predict extended-release opioid dispensing after total joint arthroplasty, and how much variation exists between providers?
Key Findings:
  • 12.1% of 229,995 patients filled extended-release opioid prescriptions within 7 days of discharge despite clinical guidelines recommending against this practice
  • Neuraxial anaesthesia (OR 0.79, 95% CI 0.74-0.84), peripheral nerve blocks (OR 0.84, 95% CI 0.79-0.89), and acute pain service involvement (OR 0.77, 95% CI 0.70-0.85) significantly reduced prescribing rates
  • Substantial variation existed between hospitals (46% of total variation) and surgeons (26%), while anaesthetist-level variation was minimal (1%)
Clinical Relevance: UK anaesthetists can reduce inappropriate extended-release opioid prescribing through regional anaesthesia techniques and acute pain service engagement, supporting Enhanced Recovery After Surgery protocols.
Limitations: Cross-sectional design cannot establish causality between anaesthetic techniques and reduced opioid prescribing patterns.

British journal of anaesthesia

Physiology, monitoring, and optimisation of perioperative tissue oxygenation: a narrative review

Meier J, Lasocki S, Meybohm P et al. · 2026 Apr 13
Study Type: Narrative review
Key Question: How can perioperative tissue oxygenation be monitored and optimised across the physiological pathway from macrocirculation to cellular metabolism?
Key Findings:
  • Tissue oxygenation involves three levels: macrocirculation (requiring adequate cardiac output and perfusion pressure), microcirculation (oxygen diffusion from capillaries), and cellular metabolism (mitochondrial oxygen consumption)
  • Most microcirculation monitoring techniques (handheld vital microscopy, NIRS, laser Doppler) are not clinically implemented, and interventions targeting microcirculation have uncertain benefit
  • Direct cellular oxygen metabolism monitoring remains experimental with no routine availability
Clinical Relevance: This review provides UK anaesthetists with a comprehensive framework for understanding tissue oxygenation physiology and available monitoring/therapeutic options, though highlights significant gaps between current practice and emerging technologies.
Limitations: As a narrative review, it provides expert opinion rather than systematic evidence synthesis of intervention effectiveness.

Study Type: Commentary

Key Findings:
  • Analysis of >1 million New York State patients demonstrates elevated risk of self-harm after non-cardiac surgery
  • Suicidal behaviour represents an under-recognised and under-studied postoperative complication
  • Current understanding of perioperative mental health factors contributing to self-harm risk remains inadequate
Clinical Relevance: UK anaesthetists should recognise perioperative mental health as part of comprehensive patient care, potentially informing preoperative assessment and postoperative follow-up protocols within NHS pathways.
