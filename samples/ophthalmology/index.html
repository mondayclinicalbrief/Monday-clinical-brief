Validation of the "FIRST-ROP" Algorithm at a Neonatal Intensive Care Unit Tertiary Center over Three Decades

Malek DA, Altamirano F, Chaaya C et al. · 2026 Apr 17
Study Type: Retrospective diagnostic comparison study
Key Question: Can the FIRST-ROP algorithm safely defer initial ROP screening to 34 weeks postmenstrual age in medium-risk preterm infants (≥27 weeks gestation, ≥800g birth weight)?
Key Findings:
  • Among 3,641 medium-risk infants over 30 years, no cases requiring treatment occurred before 34 weeks PMA, with median treatment age at 40.0 weeks (IQR 37.1-41.4)
  • FIRST-ROP algorithm reduced screening examinations by 17% per patient compared to standard protocols (p<0.001)
  • Algorithm maintained 100% sensitivity for treatment-warranted ROP detection (95% CI 99.3-100%)
Clinical Relevance: This large-scale validation supports adopting delayed ROP screening protocols in medium-risk infants, potentially reducing healthcare burden and infant stress in UK neonatal units while maintaining safety.
Limitations: Single-centre retrospective study may limit generalisability to other populations and healthcare systems.

American journal of ophthalmology

Genomic approaches to diagnosis in congenital and developmental cataract: comparison of panel versus whole genome sequencing, diagnostic yield, variant spectrum, genotype-phenotype correlations and implications for counselling

Lloyd IC, Marlowe S, Piergentili M et al. · 2026 Apr 19
Study Type: Retrospective cohort study
Key Question: Does whole genome sequencing improve diagnostic yield compared to targeted gene panels in patients with congenital cataracts?
Key Findings:
  • WGS increased diagnostic yield by 10% compared to gene panels in 119 consecutive patients with congenital cataracts
  • Overall molecular diagnosis rate was 42%, with higher rates in non-syndromic cases (51.6% vs syndromic cases)
  • Syndromic presentations accounted for 47.8% of cases, highlighting frequent multisystem involvement
Clinical Relevance: This supports adopting WGS as first-line genetic testing for congenital cataracts in NHS genetics services, given improved diagnostic rates that directly impact patient counselling, family planning, and surveillance strategies.
Limitations: Single-centre retrospective study with potential selection bias and no cost-effectiveness analysis comparing the two approaches.

American journal of ophthalmology

Ocular Symptoms Lead to Shortest Diagnostic Delay in Biopsy-Proven Giant Cell Arteritis: A Nationwide Veterans Health Study

Placide J, Phan M, Tedla S et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Retrospective multicenter cohort study
Key Question: What factors influence diagnostic delays in biopsy-proven giant cell arteritis within the US Veterans Health Administration?
Key Findings:
  • Patients with ocular symptoms (transient vision loss, visual disturbances) had shortest diagnostic delays at 8.3 days from symptom onset compared to headache (12.9 days) or systemic complaints (20.2 days; p<0.001)
  • Emergency department and eyecare providers initiated treatment significantly faster than primary care physicians
  • Mean delays were 22 days from symptom onset to first clinical visit and 11 days from visit to corticosteroid initiation
Clinical Relevance: This reinforces the importance of urgent ophthalmology assessment for suspected GCA and highlights the need for improved GCA recognition training in primary care to prevent irreversible vision loss.
Limitations: Findings from a predominantly male US veteran population may not generalise to typical UK NHS demographics or healthcare pathways.

American journal of ophthalmology

Glucagon-Like Peptide-1 Receptor Agonists and Risk of Systemic and Ocular Vascular Complications in Patients with Type 2 Diabetes and Diabetic Retinopathy

Shah J, Makwana B, Panchal K et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Retrospective, population-based cohort study
Key Question: Do GLP-1 receptor agonists reduce vascular complications in patients with type 2 diabetes and pre-existing diabetic retinopathy?
Key Findings:
  • In 30,613 matched patients, GLP-1 RAs reduced risk of myocardial infarction (HR 0.65, 95% CI 0.61-0.69), stroke (HR 0.78, 95% CI 0.74-0.83), and acute kidney injury (HR 0.68, 95% CI 0.66-0.71)
  • Ocular benefits included reduced progression to proliferative diabetic retinopathy (HR 0.78, 95% CI 0.71-0.86), retinal vein occlusions (HR 0.70, 95% CI 0.61-0.80), and neovascular glaucoma (HR 0.65, 95% CI 0.47-0.89)
  • No significant association found for retinal artery occlusions or non-arteritic ischaemic optic neuropathy
Clinical Relevance: This supports GLP-1 RA use in diabetic patients with established retinopathy for both systemic cardiovascular protection and specific retinal vascular benefits, relevant for joint diabetic eye care pathways.
Limitations: Retrospective design with potential unmeasured confounding and limited follow-up period of two years.

American journal of ophthalmology

Temporal Incidence of Dry Eye Disease, Visual Disturbances, and Ocular Pain After LASIK vs PRK

Kang S, Persad LS, Woreta FA et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Retrospective registry-based cohort study
Key Question: What are the temporal differences in postoperative complications (dry eye disease, visual disturbances, and ocular pain) between LASIK and PRK procedures?
Key Findings:
  • Dry eye disease occurred in 16.1% of LASIK eyes vs 15.6% of PRK eyes overall, with LASIK showing lower incidence at 1-3 months (IRR 0.8, 95% CI 0.7-0.9)
  • Visual disturbances were more frequent after PRK in the first month (IRR 0.3, 95% CI 0.3-0.4) but differences disappeared at later intervals
  • Ocular pain was rare overall but lower after LASIK at 3-12 months (IRR 0.5, 95% CI 0.3-0.9)
Clinical Relevance: This large-scale data helps inform patient counselling about expected complication timelines and can guide follow-up scheduling protocols in UK refractive surgery services.
Limitations: Registry-based analysis relies on coding accuracy and may underestimate mild complications not requiring clinical intervention.

Visual Impairment as a Marker of Systemic Vulnerability and Cause-Specific Mortality in U.S. Adults

Kim T, Liu JZ, Zhou J et al. · 2026 Apr 23
Study Type: Population-based cohort study
Key Question: Does visual impairment in US adults increase mortality risk, and if so, which specific causes of death drive this association?
Key Findings:
  • Visual impairment (VA worse than 20/40 in better eye) was associated with 36% increased all-cause mortality (HR 1.36, 95% CI 1.17-1.57) over 13.2 years follow-up
  • The strongest association was with non-cardiovascular, non-cancer mortality (HR 1.55, 95% CI 1.30-1.84)
  • Visual impairment increased cardiovascular mortality risk (HR 1.33, 95% CI 1.02-1.72) but showed no association with cancer mortality
Clinical Relevance: This suggests visual impairment may serve as a marker of systemic frailty in older adults, supporting integration of vision assessment into routine health monitoring within NHS chronic disease management pathways.
Limitations: As an observational study, it cannot establish causality between visual impairment and mortality outcomes.

American journal of ophthalmology

Vitamin B intake is associated with lower incidence of open-angle glaucoma: the Rotterdam Study

van Haarlem MT, van Garderen KA, De Vries VA et al. · 2026 Apr 23
Study Type: Prospective population-based cohort study
Key Question: Does dietary B-vitamin intake affect the incidence of open-angle glaucoma in a general population?
Key Findings:
  • Higher niacin (B3) intake reduced incident glaucoma risk by 57% (OR 0.43, 95% CI 0.21-0.86) comparing highest vs lowest quintiles (23.27 vs 9.98 mg/day)
  • Higher cobalamin (B12) intake reduced incident glaucoma risk by 75% (OR 0.25, 95% CI 0.12-0.52) comparing highest vs lowest quintiles (10.47 vs 0.19 µg/day)
  • Both vitamins were associated with lower IOP and B12 with thicker ganglion cell layer
Clinical Relevance: This suggests dietary B-vitamin intake may be a modifiable risk factor for glaucoma prevention, potentially informing patient counselling and dietary recommendations in high-risk individuals.
Limitations: Observational design prevents establishing causation, and dietary intake was assessed through questionnaires which may introduce measurement error.

Axial Length, Refraction, and the Language of Ophthalmology

Chang TCP, Bartlett CF, Khan AO · 2026 Apr 24
Study Type: Perspectives article/commentary
Key Question: How can ophthalmology improve terminology to better distinguish between refractive status and anatomical eye structure, particularly axial length?
Key Findings:
  • Axial length alone explains only 29% of refractive variance, while axial length-to-corneal radius ratio explains 56%, and multivariable biometric models account for >99%
  • Historical analysis reveals refractive terms (myopia/hyperopia) originally described visual function, not anatomical structure
  • Authors propose "dolichophthalmia" (long eye) and "brachyophthalmia" (short eye) as more anatomically precise alternatives to describe globe dimensions
Clinical Relevance: This addresses communication challenges in UK myopia management services where clinicians need to distinguish between refractive correction needs and structural eye changes for myopia control strategies.
Limitations: This is a perspectives piece without original data collection or validation of proposed terminology in clinical practice.
