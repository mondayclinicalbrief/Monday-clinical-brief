Annals of the rheumatic diseases

The evolving landscape of complement therapeutics in rheumatology

Holers VM · 2026 Apr 18
Study Type: Narrative review
Key Question: What is the current evidence for complement-targeted therapies in rheumatological diseases, particularly ANCA-associated vasculitis and rheumatoid arthritis?
Key Findings:
  • In ANCA-associated vasculitis, oral C5a receptor antagonists demonstrated substantial corticosteroid-sparing effects with initial clinical benefit
  • In rheumatoid arthritis, C5 and C5aR1 inhibitors showed only modest benefit despite strong biomarker and preclinical evidence
  • Both diseases appear driven by complement alternative pathway C5a generation causing target organ damage
Clinical Relevance: UK rheumatologists should be aware of emerging complement therapeutics, particularly for ANCA-associated vasculitis where steroid-sparing strategies are clinically valuable and could reduce long-term treatment burden.
Limitations: This narrative review format limits systematic assessment of evidence quality and may reflect author selection bias in study inclusion.

Musculoskeletal ultrasound findings in people with asymptomatic hyperuricemia: baseline analysis from the Transitions in Gout Research (TIGER) study using the OMERACT gout ultrasound semiquantitative scoring system

Stewart S, Gamble GD, Taylor WJ et al. · 2026 Apr 18
Study Type: Cross-sectional baseline analysis from a prospective cohort study
Key Question: What ultrasound abnormalities are present in people with asymptomatic hyperuricaemia, and how do these relate to subclinical joint damage?
Key Findings:
  • 38.7% of asymptomatic hyperuricaemic participants (n=269) had definite ultrasound features of gout (double contour and/or tophi)
  • Median OMERACT gout ultrasound score was low at 2 (IQR 0-4, maximum possible 60)
  • Double contour lesions were associated with synovial hypertrophy, while tophi correlated with erosion, synovial hypertrophy, and power Doppler activity at first MTP joints
Clinical Relevance: Identifies that over one-third of people with asymptomatic hyperuricaemia already have ultrasound evidence of urate deposition, potentially informing early intervention strategies before first gout flare in UK practice.
Limitations: Cross-sectional design cannot establish temporal relationships between ultrasound findings and progression to clinical gout.

Annals of the rheumatic diseases

Response of ultrasound-assessed synovitis to baricitinib, in monotherapy and combined with methotrexate, compared with etanercept in rheumatoid arthritis: a randomised, international, multicentre trial

Naredo E, Olivas-Vergara O, Borges PE et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Randomised controlled trial (phase IV, active-controlled, multicentre)
Key Question: Is baricitinib (alone or with methotrexate) non-inferior to etanercept plus methotrexate for ultrasound-assessed synovitis improvement in MTX-inadequate responder RA patients?
Key Findings:
  • Non-inferiority of baricitinib monotherapy and baricitinib plus MTX versus etanercept plus MTX was demonstrated for ultrasound synovitis scores at 12 weeks (P < 0.050)
  • All treatment arms showed significant clinical and ultrasound improvements from week 4 onwards
  • Changes in metalloprotease-3 levels correlated significantly with ultrasound synovitis score improvements across all groups
Clinical Relevance: This provides UK rheumatologists with evidence that baricitinib monotherapy may be a viable alternative to TNF inhibitors plus MTX in MTX-inadequate responders, potentially useful when MTX is contraindicated or poorly tolerated.
Limitations: The study used a non-inferiority design with an 80% threshold, which may not reflect clinically meaningful differences between treatments.

Winthrop KL, Kerschbaumer A, Isaacs JD et al. · 2026 Apr 23
Study Type: Consensus meeting report
Key Question: What are the most important unmet scientific needs and research priorities across major rheumatic diseases?
Key Findings:
  • Six disease groups (RA, PsA, axSpA, SLE, SSc, OA) identified convergent priorities including earlier detection/interception, validated molecular endotypes for treatment selection, and immune reset strategies with safety oversight
  • Disease-specific priorities include precision axSpA targets, fibroblast-directed RA therapies, phenotype-driven OA development with intra-articular options

Clinical Relevance: This expert consensus provides a roadmap for future rheumatology research priorities that could influence NHS commissioning decisions and clinical trial participation strategies.
Limitations: Consensus opinions from expert investigators may not fully represent broader clinical community perspectives or patient priorities.

Annals of the rheumatic diseases

A pathogenic gut lipoglycan drives systemic thromboinflammation in lupus nephritis

Amarnani A, Rivera CF, Cornwell M et al. · 2027 Apr 23
Study Type: Cohort study with mechanistic validation in murine models
Key Question: Does Ruminococcus gnavus gut expansion drive lupus nephritis flares through a specific lipoglycan toxin mechanism?
Key Findings:
  • Lupus nephritis patients with gut R. gnavus expansions showed significantly elevated platelet factor 4 and neutrophil extracellular traps (NETs) compared to those without expansions
  • High serum IgG antibodies against R. gnavus-specific lipoglycan correlated with thromboinflammatory profiles in both pilot and validation cohorts
  • Murine studies confirmed lipoglycan injection caused megakaryocytosis, platelet activation, and tubulointerstitial injury with NETosis via Toll-like receptor 2-dependent mechanisms
Clinical Relevance: This identifies a novel microbiome-driven endotype of lupus nephritis that could inform targeted therapeutic strategies and biomarker development for NHS lupus services.
Limitations: Limited cohort sizes and mechanistic extrapolation from murine models to human disease pathophysiology requires validation.

Arthritis & rheumatology (Hoboken, N.J.)

Integrative Multi-omics Approaches Identify Biomarkers Associated with Progression from Arthralgia to Rheumatoid Arthritis

Li M, Han Y, Zhan M et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Prospective cohort study
Key Question: What immunological biomarkers can identify patients with arthralgia who will progress to rheumatoid arthritis?
Key Findings:
  • 18.4% of arthralgia-at-risk patients developed RA within 24 months, with reduced regulatory T cells (Tregs) and lower Treg/Th17 ratios preceding conversion
  • Treg/Th17 ratio outperformed anti-CCP antibodies in distinguishing at-risk from self-limiting arthralgia (AUC 0.734 vs 0.611), particularly in ACPA-negative patients (AUC 0.729)
  • Combined biomarker panel (Tregs, anti-CCP, Treg/Th17 ratio) achieved best discrimination performance (AUC 0.783)
Clinical Relevance: This could help UK rheumatologists stratify arthralgia patients in early arthritis clinics, potentially identifying high-risk individuals for closer monitoring or preventive interventions before established RA develops.
Limitations: The study lacks validation in an independent cohort and Treg measurement requires specialised flow cytometry not routinely available in most NHS laboratories.

Single-cell transcriptomic analysis on multi-subtypes of idiopathic inflammatory myopathy reveals pathological hallmarks associated with stromal stem and immune cells in damaged skeletal muscles

Wang Z, Li Y, Li X et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Single-cell RNA sequencing analysis
Key Question: What are the cellular and molecular pathogenic mechanisms across different subtypes of idiopathic inflammatory myopathies?
Key Findings:
  • Type IIa/x myofiber reduction and decreased MEF2C transcription factor expression were common across inflammatory IIM subtypes, with IFN-I signaling selectively hyperactivated in dermatomyositis but not IMNM
  • M1-like macrophages interact with muscle cells via TNF signaling, recruited by CXCL10+ fibroblasts through the CCL19-CCR7/CCRL2 axis
  • Muscle stem cells (MuSCs1 and Myoblasts1) show disrupted regeneration pathways, with peripheral CD14+ monocytes exhibiting high interferon activity before polarising to M1 phenotype in muscle
Clinical Relevance: This provides mechanistic insights into IIM subtype-specific pathology that could inform targeted therapeutic approaches and biomarker development for UK rheumatology practice.
Limitations: Small sample sizes (n=3-5 per subtype) may limit generalisability of findings across the heterogeneous IIM population.
