Developing a Virtual Research Environment in the Parkinson's Progression Markers Initiative: myPPMI

Stanley CE, Fabrizio K, McMahon BA et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Technology development/implementation study
Key Question: How can a virtual research environment enhance participant engagement and data collection efficiency in Parkinson's disease research?
Key Findings:
  • myPPMI platform integrates education, screening, e-consent, motor/non-motor assessments, and biospecimen referral into a single virtual environment
  • Platform enables precision recruitment with real-time eligibility matching and standardized low-burden data capture across multiple studies
  • Cloud-native architecture supports multi-country deployment with daily analytics and tiered support model
Clinical Relevance: This virtual research framework could inform development of similar platforms for UK neurological research, potentially improving patient recruitment and reducing research participation burden within NHS trusts.
Limitations: No quantitative outcomes or comparative effectiveness data provided to assess platform performance versus traditional research methods.

Stroke Etiologies With Intravenous Thrombolysis before Thrombectomy and Functional Outcomes in Anterior Circulation Large Vessel Occlusion

Guo C, Cavalcante F, Yang S et al. · 2026 Apr 23
Study Type: Individual participant data meta-analysis of 6 RCTs
Key Question: Does stroke etiology modify the benefit of intravenous thrombolysis before thrombectomy compared to thrombectomy alone in large vessel occlusion patients presenting directly to EVT-capable centres?
Key Findings:
  • No significant treatment effect modification by stroke etiology overall (p=0.60 for interaction) across 2,313 patients with large artery atherosclerosis (19%), cardioembolism (42%), or other/undetermined etiology (39%)
  • Patients with other/undetermined etiology showed benefit from IVT+EVT (adjusted cOR 1.34, 95% CI 1.05-1.69 for better 90-day functional outcome)
  • No safety differences by etiology or atrial fibrillation status
Clinical Relevance: This supports current UK stroke guidelines recommending IVT before thrombectomy regardless of presumed stroke mechanism, though the isolated benefit in cryptogenic strokes warrants further investigation.
Limitations: The benefit observed specifically in the other/undetermined etiology subgroup requires confirmation in future studies.

Brain : a journal of neurology

Neuropathological correlates of age and sex differences in 18F-flortaucipir PET

Coomans EM, Smith R, Pawlik D et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Cross-sectional analysis using three independent cohorts (PET-only, autopsy-only, and PET-to-autopsy validation)
Key Question: Do older age and male sex affect flortaucipir-PET sensitivity for detecting tau pathology in Alzheimer's disease, and what are the neuropathological correlates?
Key Findings:
  • Flortaucipir-PET showed 100% specificity for Braak V-VI pathology but sensitivity decreased markedly with older age (<83y: 92% vs ≥83y: 42%) and in males (females: 85% vs males: 48%)
  • Lower PET sensitivity correlated with genuinely lower tangle density at autopsy in older individuals (β=-0.38, p<0.001) and males
  • PET SUVr accurately reflected tangle density across all age and sex groups, indicating the tracer performs consistently but detects real biological differences
Clinical Relevance: UK clinicians should interpret negative flortaucipir-PET scans cautiously in older patients and males, as these may reflect lower tangle density below detection threshold rather than absence of significant tau pathology.
Limitations: Median PET-to-autopsy interval of 2.6 months may allow tau progression between imaging and neuropathological assessment.

Satellite glial GLRX3 drives ageing-biased neuropathic pain via HMGB1

Yang Y, Zhao B, Liu X et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Preclinical experimental study with clinical cohort validation
Key Question: What molecular mechanisms drive the age-related persistence of oxaliplatin-induced peripheral neuropathy?
Key Findings:
  • Satellite glial cells in aged mice show selective upregulation of glutaredoxin-3 (GLRX3), which deglutathionylates HMGB1, converting it into a TLR4 agonist that sustains neuropathic pain
  • Clinical cohort confirmed advanced age associates with higher chronic neuropathy incidence, with declining serum protein S-glutathionylation levels correlating inversely with pain duration
  • Targeted GLRX3 knockdown or pharmacological interventions (γ-glutamylcysteine, TAK-242) reversed pain phenotypes specifically in aged models
Clinical Relevance: This research identifies circulating protein S-glutathionylation as a potential age-stratified biomarker for chronic neuropathy and suggests targeted therapeutic approaches for elderly patients with chemotherapy-induced neuropathy—relevant given the aging UK cancer population.
Limitations: Findings primarily from murine models with limited validation in human cohorts may not fully translate to clinical practice.

Brain : a journal of neurology

The impact of the COVID-19 pandemic on the incidence and clinical profiles of Guillain-Barré syndrome in Japan

Suichi T, Misawa S, Ukita S et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Nationwide cohort study
Key Question: Did the COVID-19 pandemic alter the incidence and clinical characteristics of Guillain-Barré syndrome in Japan?
Key Findings:
  • GBS incidence decreased by 9% during the pandemic (1.49 to 1.28 per 100,000; RR 0.91, 95% CI 0.83-0.99)
  • Pandemic-era patients were older (median 56 vs 53 years), had fewer antecedent infections (38.2% vs 24.5% with no preceding illness), and more demyelinating subtypes (37.8% vs 32.6%)
  • Time to nadir was longer during the pandemic (8 vs 7 days), but 6-month outcomes were similar
Clinical Relevance: This provides evidence for how infection control measures during COVID-19 affected GBS epidemiology, with implications for understanding triggers and potentially informing future pandemic responses in neurology services.
Limitations: The study relies on voluntary questionnaire responses from hospitals, which may introduce reporting bias and underestimate true case numbers.

Brain : a journal of neurology

Default mode network failure across the Alzheimer's disease spectrum

Corriveau-Lecavalier N, Dicks E, Martin PR et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Longitudinal cohort study
Key Question: How do default mode network connectivity changes relate to amyloid accumulation and predict conversion across the Alzheimer's disease spectrum?
Key Findings:
  • In 1,451 participants (2,763 timepoints), both hyper- and hypoconnectivity emerged across DMN subsystems with increasing amyloid burden
  • DMN connectivity patterns predicted conversion to amyloid positivity, MCI, and dementia, independent of age, APOE4 status, sex, and education
  • Functional network disruption preceded detectable amyloidosis on PET imaging in clinically unimpaired individuals
Clinical Relevance: This suggests functional MRI could potentially identify at-risk individuals before amyloid becomes detectable, offering earlier intervention opportunities within NHS memory services.
Limitations: The study does not address whether these connectivity patterns can be reliably measured at individual patient level for clinical application.

Brain : a journal of neurology

Collapse of feed-forward inhibition underpins hyperexcitability in GABAA gain-of-function epilepsy

McKenzie CE, Aung KP, Dos Santos AB et al. · 2026 Apr 22
Study Type: Experimental mouse model study with electrophysiology and computational modelling
Key Question: How do gain-of-function GABAA receptor variants cause hyperexcitability and seizures in developmental and epileptic encephalopathies?
Key Findings:
  • β3E77K GOF variant mice showed spike-wave discharges, increased seizure susceptibility, and cortical hyperexcitability, with vigabatrin worsening and valproate improving seizures (matching human phenotype)
  • Enhanced GABAA currents in cortical interneurons caused use-dependent collapse of feed-forward inhibition, paradoxically increasing pyramidal neuron excitability
  • Computational modelling confirmed that stronger GABAA synapses between interneurons reduces overall inhibitory drive to pyramidal cells
Clinical Relevance: This study explains the counterintuitive mechanism whereby enhanced GABAA function causes epilepsy, providing a preclinical model for testing treatments in GOF GABAA receptor epilepsies increasingly recognised in UK epilepsy genetics services.
Limitations: Mouse model findings require validation in human tissue and clinical translation of therapeutic implications.
