Molecular mechanism of cleavage at R271 during prothrombin activation revealed by cryo-EM

Stojanovski B, Mohammed BM, Basore K et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Structural biology study using cryo-electron microscopy
Key Question: How does the prothrombinase complex achieve the second proteolytic cleavage at R271 to convert meizothrombin to active thrombin?
Key Findings:
  • Cryo-EM structure at 3.8 Å resolution revealed the molecular mechanism of R271 cleavage in meizothrombin activation
  • Cleavage site switching from R320 to R271 requires significant reorientation (>20 Å movement) of the protease domain rather than conformational changes
  • Molecular contacts primarily involve protease domains of meizothrombin and factor Xa, validating previous biochemical studies
Clinical Relevance: This structural insight into thrombin generation could inform development of more targeted anticoagulants or procoagulant therapies for bleeding disorders managed in UK haematology services.
Limitations: The study used a truncated form of meizothrombin which may not fully represent the native activation process.

Modulation of the E3-ubiquitin ligase RNF217 alters ferroportin levels in enterocytes and iron loading in murine hemochromatosis

Yu Y, Jiang L, Lin Z et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Experimental study using genetically modified mouse models
Key Question: Can overexpression of the E3 ubiquitin ligase RNF217 ameliorate iron overload in hereditary haemochromatosis by modulating ferroportin levels independently of hepcidin?
Key Findings:
  • Both global and intestine-specific RNF217 overexpression rescued the haemochromatosis phenotype in Hjv-/- knockout mice, with duodenal enterocytes identified as the primary site of action
  • Intestinal RNF217 overexpression significantly reduced serum and organ iron accumulation through direct modulation of ferroportin protein levels
  • The iron-reducing effects occurred independently of hepcidin levels, suggesting a hepcidin-independent pathway for iron regulation
Clinical Relevance: This research identifies a potential novel therapeutic target for hereditary haemochromatosis that could bypass hepcidin deficiency, offering hope for patients with severe iron overload who may not respond adequately to current treatments.
Limitations: This is preclinical mouse model research requiring validation in human studies before clinical translation.

Harnessing Neoantigen-specific T Cells for Precision Immunotherapy in Hematologic Malignancies

Kandel A, Cha SC, Kwak LW · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Narrative review
Key Question: How can neoantigen-specific T-cell immunotherapies be developed and optimised for treating hematological malignancies?
Key Findings:
  • Neoantigen-specific therapies offer superior tumour selectivity and reduced off-target toxicity compared to tumour-associated antigen approaches by targeting HLA-restricted, tumour-derived peptides
  • Early-phase clinical trials demonstrate safety and feasibility across multiple haematological cancers, though efficacy and durability data are still emerging
  • Advances in immunogenomics and high-resolution sequencing are accelerating identification of both personalised and recurrent neoantigens for TCR-engineered T-cell development
Clinical Relevance: This provides UK haematology clinicians with a framework for understanding an emerging immunotherapy modality that may offer more precise alternatives to current CAR-T approaches, particularly relevant as the NHS expands cellular therapy programmes.
Limitations: As a review article, it synthesises existing literature rather than presenting new clinical data on efficacy outcomes.

IFNγ Drives Long-Term Bone Marrow Niche Dysfunction Following Doxorubicin-Based Chemotherapy

Li X, Aguilar-Navarro AG, Nader M et al. · 2026 Apr 20
Study Type: Preclinical experimental study with human correlative data
Key Question: What mechanisms drive long-term bone marrow niche dysfunction following doxorubicin-based chemotherapy in cancer survivors?
Key Findings:
  • Doxorubicin chemotherapy caused loss of arteriolar vasculature, impaired mesenchymal stromal cell differentiation, trabecular bone loss, and reduced HSC maintenance capacity in murine models
  • CD8⁺ T cell-derived IFNγ was identified as a key driver of chronic inflammatory remodeling; IFNγ blockade partially restored vascular structure and adipogenic differentiation
  • Human bone marrow samples from leukaemia patients showed similar MSC lineage alterations, inflammatory pathway upregulation, and CD8⁺ memory T cell expansion post-chemotherapy
Clinical Relevance: This research identifies potential therapeutic targets (IFNγ signaling) to prevent long-term skeletal and haematopoietic complications in chemotherapy survivors, which could improve quality of life outcomes for UK cancer patients.
Limitations: Preclinical findings require validation in larger human cohorts before clinical translation.

Ahamed J · 2026 Apr 20

Key Question: Does platelet-derived TGF-β1 maintain immune tolerance in immune thrombocytopenic purpura (ITP) through regulatory T cell (Treg) modulation?
Key Findings:
  • Authors challenge previous findings by Mei et al., arguing that reported TGF-β1 levels were implausibly high and likely due to ex vivo measurement artifacts
  • Knockout mouse studies failed to reproduce platelet-derived TGF-β1-driven Treg deficiency
  • ITP remission maintenance appears to involve TGF-β1 sources other than platelets
Clinical Relevance: This challenges current understanding of platelet-derived immunomodulation in ITP, potentially redirecting research towards alternative TGF-β1 sources for therapeutic targeting in UK ITP patients.
Limitations: Limited methodological details provided in this brief commentary format.

PF4/Heparin-Nonbinding Platelet-Activating Antibodies in Heparin-Induced Thrombocytopenia

Zhou L, Cao A, Zhu W et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Laboratory-based mechanistic study with single-cell cloning and mouse model validation
Key Question: Do clinically significant HIT antibodies exist that are undetectable by standard PF4/heparin ELISA but still cause platelet activation?
Key Findings:
  • ELISA-negative but PEA-positive (ELISA⁻PEA⁺) antibodies comprised 65±19% of total platelet-activating activity in 11 confirmed HIT patients
  • Single-cell cloning identified 17 ELISA⁻PEA⁺ versus 6 ELISA⁺PEA⁺ antibody-producing B-cell clones across 7 patients
  • These antibodies induced thrombocytopenia in humanised mouse models and showed identical functional characteristics to conventional HIT antibodies
Clinical Relevance: This challenges the reliability of negative PF4/heparin ELISA results to exclude HIT in UK practice, potentially explaining cases of clinical HIT with negative serology.
Limitations: Small patient cohort (11 patients) and the clinical prevalence of ELISA⁻PEA⁺ antibodies in suspected HIT remains undefined.

Refined classification and phenotype-driven analysis of PIEZO1 variants in hereditary red cell and iron disorders

Rosato BE, Marra R, Martone S et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Retrospective genetic analysis with phenotype-genotype correlation
Key Question: How can PIEZO1 variant classification be improved to better predict clinical phenotypes in hereditary stomatocytosis?
Key Findings:
  • Analysis of 2,565 PIEZO1 variants using integrated ACMG guidelines and structural annotation enabled reclassification of nearly 1,000 variants of uncertain significance
  • Pathogenic variants clustered non-randomly in functionally constrained domains (Anchor, Inner helix, C-terminal regions)
  • Three distinct phenotypic clusters identified in 176 DHS1 patients, ranging from severe hemolysis with iron overload to subclinical presentations
Clinical Relevance: This refined variant classification system could improve diagnostic accuracy for UK patients with unexplained hemolytic anemia and iron overload, potentially reducing diagnostic delays and enabling more targeted monitoring strategies.
Limitations: Study relies heavily on computational predictions and may not capture all functional consequences of variants in this complex mechanosensitive channel.

Mosunetuzumab plus polatuzumab vedotin in relapsed/refractory MCL after BTK inhibitor therapy: a phase 2 study

Budde LE, Kamdar M, Assouline SE et al. · 2026 Apr 21
Study Type: Phase 2 clinical trial
Key Question: What is the efficacy and safety of mosunetuzumab plus polatuzumab vedotin in patients with relapsed/refractory mantle cell lymphoma who have previously received BTK inhibitor therapy?
Key Findings:
  • Overall response rate was 88.1% (95% CI 74.4-96.0%) with complete response in 78.6% (95% CI 63.2-89.7%) in 42 patients with median 3 prior therapies
  • Median progression-free survival was 18.6 months (95% CI 13.9-not estimable) at 15.9 months median follow-up
  • Cytokine release syndrome occurred in 42.9% of patients but was limited to Grade 1-2 severity only
Clinical Relevance: This bispecific-ADC combination offers promising salvage therapy for heavily pre-treated MCL patients, including those with high-risk features and prior CAR-T therapy, addressing a significant unmet need in UK haematology practice.
Limitations: Single-arm design without comparator group limits interpretation of efficacy relative to other available salvage therapies.
